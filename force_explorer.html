An interactive companion to the Salish Marine Map. Set an idealized channel, a wind, and a tidal current, then watch how the wave steepness and the wave force respond — especially to a shift in wind direction (which changes the fetch) or a change in current. Every number comes from the same literature-based formulas the map uses; see the physics write-up (PDF).

How “force” is defined here — and the height/period scaling

Your instructor’s rule that wave force “scales sharply with height and period” is real physics. Two rigorous quantities capture it, and the model reports both:

Energy density  E = 1⁄16 · ρg·Hs²   energy per m² of sea surface — grows with the square of height (twice as high ⇒ ~4× the energy) Wave power  P = ρg²⁄(64π) · Hs²·Tp ≈ 0.49 · Hs² · Tp  (kW per metre of crest)   rate energy is delivered — rises with height squared and with period; this is the “force” headline

So a wave twice as high delivers roughly four times the power, and a longer period delivers it faster — exactly the effect a paddler feels. When a current opposes the waves, the model first raises the height by the steepening factor before computing E and P, so the force readout already includes the current’s effect. The genuine peak impact of a breaking wave slamming a hull is a harder, hull-specific problem (Goda 2000; Cuomo et al. 2010) that scales with crest particle-velocity² — tied back to steepness. This tool reports the well-founded energy/power, not a precise slam load; treat the “force” number as a relative index. Full derivation and references are in the PDF.

Idealized single-channel model for understanding — not a site forecast. Deep-water wind sea (JONSWAP fetch-limited, Pierson–Moskowitz cap) steepened by an opposing current (wave-action, blocking at c₀⁄4). No swell, no build-up time, no shoaling. Advisory only — not for navigation.
