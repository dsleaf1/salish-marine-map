How “force” is defined here — and the height/period scaling

Your instructor’s rule that wave force “scales sharply with height and period” is real physics. Two rigorous quantities capture it, and the model reports both:

Energy density  E = 1⁄16 · ρg·Hs²   energy per m² of sea surface — grows with the square of height (twice as high ⇒ ~4× the energy) Wave power  P = ρg²⁄(64π) · Hs²·Tp ≈ 0.49 · Hs² · Tp  (kW per metre of crest)   rate energy is delivered — rises with height squared and with period; this is the “force” headline

So a wave twice as high delivers roughly four times the power, and a longer period delivers it faster — exactly the effect a paddler feels. When a current opposes the waves, the model first raises the height by the steepening factor before computing E and P, so the force readout already includes the current’s effect. The genuine peak impact of a breaking wave slamming a hull is a harder, hull-specific problem (Goda 2000; Cuomo et al. 2010) that scales with crest particle-velocity² — tied back to steepness. This tool reports the well-founded energy/power, not a precise slam load; treat the “force” number as a relative index. Full derivation and references are in the PDF.

The blocking “cliff”, and why the spectral model is more realistic

Set a long narrow channel (say 31 km × 1.5 km), a 13 kt wind on-axis and a ~1.6 kt opposing current, and the monochromatic model does something unphysical: a wind shift of just 0–4° flips the water from mild to hazardous. Two things combine to cause it.

1 · Fetch collapses over a few degrees. For a channel the idealized fetch is min(L⁄cosΔ, (W⁄2)⁄sinΔ). The two terms cross at Δ = atan((W⁄2)⁄L) — only ~1.4° for this channel — and past it the fetch falls off as 1⁄sinΔ, dropping from 31 km to ~5 km within 8°. (That crossover angle grows with width, which is exactly why the anomaly eases in a wider channel.)

2 · The steepening formula is singular at blocking. As the opposing current nears a quarter of the wave speed, the monochromatic factor S = x3.5⁄√(2−x)·S₀ diverges. The shrinking fetch lowers the wave period, hence the wave speed, hence the blocking current — so the fixed current walks the operating point straight into that singularity. The fetch is the trigger; the singularity is the amplifier, and it lives in the core model, not just this channel geometry.

How real calculations avoid it — the spectral view. A real wind sea is not one wave but a spectrum of frequencies, and each frequency has its own speed and blocks at its own current (short, slow waves first). As the current builds, the high-frequency tail blocks and breaks progressively rather than all at once, so the transition is a gradual ramp, not a cliff. Toggle Spectral and watch the peak sensitivity drop (roughly halved here) and the dashed monochromatic curve reveal the sharp lobe behind it. This is the essence of how spectral action-balance models (SWAN — Booij et al. 1999; WAVEWATCH III) and the random-wave blocking measurements of Chawla & Kirby (2002) behave. A complementary fix on the geometry side is an effective (direction-averaged) fetch (Saville 1954; CERC Shore Protection Manual), which smooths the fetch-vs-direction spike — though a fixed ±45° window over-penalizes a narrow channel.

A second anomaly, and the deeper missing piece. The first spectral version simply deleted the energy of blocked components (“they broke, the energy is gone”). That produced its own absurdity, found by testing: pushing the current from 2.7 to 2.9 kt made the reported wave height drop (0.35 → 0.18 m) and the hazard grade fall — the strongest water read as the safest. The model was answering the wrong question: it reported the transmitted remnant (what survives beyond the blocking barrier) when the paddler’s question is what the sea looks like at the blocking zone — where the energy piles up and the water is a standing, breaking chop. What the equations were missing is what regularizes real seas: breaking as a saturation, not a deletion. In a full model (SWAN’s action balance) wind input, current transport and breaking dissipation act together, so at blocking the sea saturates at the breaking limit instead of vanishing.

The fix used here: every spectral component’s height is capped at the 1/7 steepness limit for its local, current-shortened wavelength (af ≤ Smax⁄(S₀·x²)). At blocking (x = 2) that cap is exactly the blocked-component saturation, so the energy is continuous across the blocking threshold — no cliff, no vanishing. Past full blocking the height settles at the breaking-limited chop (≈ 1/7 of a quarter of the original wavelength) — the tide-rip sea state — and the hazard grade is monotone: more opposing current can never read as safer (verified over 100 parameter sweeps). The consequence gate likewise uses the running maximum over the approach to the blocking point, weighted up for breaking water.

The spectral option remains a transparent approximation (a Pierson–Moskowitz spread with per-component blocking and breaking saturation), meant to build intuition, not a calibrated spectral solver. Still missing, honestly: local wave generation on the current (wind blowing against a 3 kt current sees a faster relative wind and keeps making new chop in situ — SWAN can include this; we don't), Doppler frequency shifts, and the spatial structure of the blocking zone.

Why an oblique wind can’t be worse than a head-on one — directional components

The third anomaly (found by field testing this page). In a 47 × 5 km channel with 14 kt of wind and a 1 kt opposing ebb, the single-ray model rated a 25°-oblique wind “rough” (0.37 m of short chop, 56% of blocking) while the head-on wind — same speed, far more fetch, far bigger waves — rated only “mild”. A small veer of the forecast wind flipped the map’s verdict in the wrong direction.

Why that is internally inconsistent. A wind sea’s short-wave tail saturates at a level set by the wind, nearly independent of fetch (Phillips saturation — the reason the JONSWAP spectrum’s high-frequency face is universal). Computing it from the model’s own spectrum: the head-on sea carries 0.22 m of height in the 1.9–2.4 s band — more than the 0.16 m the oblique sea carries there. The head-on case contains the oblique case’s chop, plus 0.8 m of longer waves on top. It cannot be less hazardous.

What was missing: waves don’t all travel downwind. The single ray sends every joule of wave energy exactly along the wind with one geometric fetch. Real channel winds raise a component sea in every direction: energy travelling at angle δ off the wind is driven by the wind component U·cos δ over the fetch in that direction. In a long channel this is why an oblique wind still builds its dominant waves along the axis — the “slanting fetch” effect, standard in restricted-fetch engineering (Seymour 1977; Donelan 1980) and confirmed by field measurements in narrow bays (Pettersson, Kahma & Tuomi 2010, J. Phys. Oceanogr.).

The fix (the “Sea model” toggle): the display follows the dominant component (largest period — Donelan’s criterion), so the reported wave height no longer collapses from 0.98 m to 0.37 m on a 25° veer; and the hazard takes the worst band over all components, because a mixed sea is as dangerous as its worst subsystem — an energy-weighted average lets a big benign swell dilute (hide) steep chop. With this, the head-on case is rated by its own embedded oblique chop and can never rank below the oblique case; the hazard stays monotone in current (a maximum of monotone functions) and varies smoothly with wind direction. It is a conservative screening treatment: each component is assessed at its full fetch-limited size.

Quartering seas — the directional hazard the steepness band can’t see (separate model)

The gap (found by field review). Steepness measures whether the sea breaks; it says nothing about the angle the waves hit a kayak — and capsize is a product of both: danger ≈ breaking × bad-angle A non-breaking wave, however large, mostly rolls under the hull; a breaking wave on the beam is the capsizer (heavy-weather literature: breaking height ≳ the hull’s beam ⇒ knockdown). The IMO’s guidance to masters (MSC.1/Circ.1228) defines the dangerous heading band as 135–225° off head sea — stern-quartering and following — where surf-riding and broaching live. For a kayak, stern-quartering is the worst single heading: it stacks the following-sea broach (the boat slews toward beam-on; past “11 o’clock” you can’t straighten it) on the beam-sea roll, while denying the paddler a pre-committed brace side. The steepness band rates a head-on breaking sea and the same sea on your stern quarter identically; this panel is the missing directional half.

Why a separate model, not a change to the band. Two reasons. First, the steepness/blocking chain is validated (monotone current sweeps, ray parity, audited closed forms) — folding an angle term into its band would change every verdict and risk reintroducing the anomalies the toggles preserve for teaching. Second, encounter geometry is a per-heading question: the same sea is mild for the paddler heading into it and hazardous for the one running before it — so the honest output is a rose over your heading, not one number. The two panels deliberately disagree in places; each is right about its own question.

The model. For a paddler course t, every wave component the sea model already computes is scored and the worst governs: Q(t) = max over components [ W(angle off bow) · breaking-proximity · size ] × confused-sea factor W anchors (literature-informed; calibration targets for the expert replay reviews): head 0.15 · bow-quarter 0.40 · beam 0.85 · stern-quarter 1.00 · following 0.55, linearly interpolated. Breaking-proximity ramps on the component’s current-steepened steepness from 0.04 to 0.12 (and takes the breaking fraction in spectral mode) — so quartering hazard materializes when opposing current steepens the sea. Both breaking-proximity and size (envelope height 0.15→0.5 m) are taken as a per-component running-max over currents 0…now, the same envelope principle as the steepness consequence gate — this keeps Q monotone in opposing current by construction even where a following-current component correctly flattens (a paddler at the rip meets the peak of the transition, not its flattened tail). The spread factor escalates ×1.0→1.3 when the wind sea itself spans ≥ 40° of direction (measured from calm-sea component heights, so it cannot create current inversions): a broad, short-crested sea gives the paddler no single side to brace. Note this is the angular width of one wind sea, not two crossing swells — the idealized channel has no swell input; genuine crossing-sea rogue-wave danger (40–60° between systems maximizes it; a ship-accident study found most losses in crossing seas) needs the multi-modal real sea and belongs in the map port, not here. In single-ray mode there is one component, so no spread can register — another reason the directional-components sea model is the default.

Why wind and current pick out the quartering case. The two forcings act on different axes: the wind sets the waves’ direction (pulled along-channel by slanting fetch), while the along-axis current sets their breaking. So the most dangerous kayak cell — an oblique wind over an opposing axial current — is a steep, breaking, quartering sea, and it is not the maximum-steepness case (that is the head-on, full-fetch wind). A steepness-only model under-rates exactly this cell; watch the two panels split when you veer the wind off-axis with an opposing ebb.

Honest limits. The W anchors are expert-judgment numbers grounded in ship guidance and coaching consensus, not measured kayak hydrodynamics — the replay review (winter validation) is the instrument for calibrating them. No surf-riding/encounter-resonance term (wave speed vs paddler speed), no paddler-skill term, and the transit pills assume travel along the channel axis; the rose shows every other course.
