Wind, fetch and current: the physics of wave steepness

Working notes for the Unified Marine Map (steepness layer) · draft, revised as we go

This note explains, for a curious non-physicist, how the map turns a wind forecast and a current forecast into a steepness hazard — and why the answer is often counter-intuitive: a modest shift in wind direction, or a small change in current, can matter more than a big change in wind speed. Every equation below is the one the live map actually evaluates, and the two charts are drawn from those same formulas. It closes with the open question we want to explore next: the force a steep wave puts on a kayak.

1 · It is steepness, not height, that tips a boat

A wave’s danger is better measured by its steepness — height divided by wavelength — than by its height alone.

S = H ⁄ L   a 1 m swell 100 m long (S = 1/100) is a gentle roll; a 1 m wave 7 m long (S = 1/7) is a breaking wall

Deep-water gravity waves become unstable and break when steepness reaches the limiting value found by Michell (1893) and Stokes for the highest progressive wave, where the crest sharpens to a 120° angle:

Smax = H ⁄ L ≈ 1 ⁄ 7 = 0.143   in a real irregular sea a significant steepness near 1/10 already means many crests are breaking

The map therefore reports steepness in four bands: mild (S ≳ 0.05), caution (≳ 0.075), rough (≳ 0.10) and hazardous / breaking (≳ 0.143).

2 · How wind builds a sea — and why fetch is the lever

Wind pushes on the water and waves grow. Two things cap how big they get: the wind speed, and the fetch — the distance of open water the wind crosses before reaching you. Near an upwind shore the sea is small; the further open water stretches upwind, the more the waves build, until they reach a “fully developed” size for that wind and grow no more. A breeze down the length of a strait raises a real sea; the same breeze across a narrow channel raises only ripples.

That is why the map computes fetch directionally: at every water cell it looks upwind and measures how far open water runs before it hits land, separately for 16 compass directions. When the forecast wind blows from a given direction, the map uses the fetch in that direction — so the same wind speed produces very different seas depending only on which way it blows.

3 · How the wave height (Hs) and period (Tp) are derived

The map uses the standard fetch-limited growth laws — the JONSWAP field-experiment fit (Hasselmann et al., 1973) — capped at the Pierson–Moskowitz fully-developed sea (Pierson & Moskowitz, 1964). With wind speed U (m/s), fetch F (m) and gravity g = 9.81 m/s²:

Hs = 4·U·√( 1.6×10⁻⁷ · F ⁄ g )   significant wave height — grows with wind, and with the square-root of fetch Tp = 1 ⁄ [ 3.5·(g⁄U)·(gF⁄U²)−0.33 ]   peak period — longer fetch gives longer, faster waves Hs ≤ 0.24·U²⁄g  ,  Tp ≤ 0.72·U   fully-developed ceilings (Pierson–Moskowitz) — the flat tops in the chart above

The subtle point: height and period both rise with fetch, but the period rises faster. A longer fetch makes waves taller and longer — and longer waves are less steep and harder for a current to trip. So a bigger sea is not automatically a rougher one, as §5 shows.

4 · How an opposing current steepens — and blocks — the sea

A deep-water wave travels at a speed set by its period, and carries a length to match:

c₀ = g·Tp ⁄ 2π  (wave speed) ,  L₀ = c₀·Tp (wavelength)   initial steepness S₀ = Hs ⁄ L₀

Only the part of the current pointing into the waves matters. With θ the angle between the current’s set and the direction the wind blows from,

Uopp = Vcurrent·cos θ   head-on is fully opposing; a cross-current barely counts; a following current does nothing

A wave riding onto an opposing current keeps its period but is forced to slow and shorten; conserving its wave-action it grows taller (Longuet-Higgins & Stewart, 1961; Phillips, 1977). Both effects raise steepness. Writing α = −Uopp ⁄ c₀ and the shortening factor x = k₀ ⁄ k from the wave-on-current dispersion relation, the steepness climbs as

S ≈ ( x3.5 ⁄ √(2−x) )·S₀

When the opposing current reaches a quarter of the wave speed the waves can no longer advance; they stop, pile up and break. This is the classic wave-blocking limit (Chawla & Kirby, 2002):

Uopp ≥ c₀ ⁄ 4   short (slow) waves have a low c₀, so they block at a modest current — the reason short-fetch chop is dangerous

6 · The open question: from steepness to force

Steepness tells you a wave is likely to break and stack up — but a paddler feels a force. Two physically grounded quantities scale that force, and both rise fast with height:

Energy density  E = ⅛·ρ·g·H²   energy per unit sea-surface area — scales with the square of height Energy flux (power)  P = E·cg ∝ H²·T   rate energy is delivered — rises with height squared AND period (group speed cg = gT/4π)

So a wave twice as high carries roughly four times the energy, and a longer period delivers it faster — which is the grain of truth in the instructor’s “force scales sharply with height and period.” The peak impact of a breaking or near-breaking wave on a hull is harder: it depends on the water-particle velocity at the crest (itself ∝ steepness × celerity) and on slamming dynamics studied in coastal engineering (Goda, 2000; Cuomo et al., 2010). Building a defensible, literature-validated force/energy index — and an interactive graph of how wind direction, fetch and current move both steepness and that index together — is the next step. This document will grow to cover it.
